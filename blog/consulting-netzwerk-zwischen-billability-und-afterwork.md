# Networking in consulting: somewhere between billability and after-work beer

Consulting has a strange rhythm.

On paper, the job is about projects, delivery, utilization, certifications, progression, and all the other words that appear reliably in performance conversations. In practice, the job is also about energy: staying curious, staying relevant, and staying connected to people who care about the same topics.

That is one reason I never wanted work to end with closing the laptop after eight hours and disappearing into silence.

I want to enjoy the field I work in. I want to keep learning. I want to talk to people about architecture decisions, new tooling, AI, cloud governance, platform operations, and occasionally the latest "hot new thing" that is either genuinely useful or will quietly disappear by next quarter.

That, to me, is part of living the consulting job rather than merely surviving it.

## The performance side is real

Let us not romanticize it too much.

Consulting demands measurable output. Performance matters. Billability matters. Progression matters. At some point, everyone asks some variation of the same question:

How do I move forward?

- better customer delivery?
- deeper technical expertise?
- certifications?
- internal business development?
- land-and-expand with a client?

All of that can matter. None of it fully answers the more personal question: what actually makes me better in a way that lasts?

One underrated answer is simple: better conversations.

## A network is more than lead generation with nicer lighting

Social media loves to sell networking as if every interaction should be a funnel.

Meet the right people. Build your brand. Capture opportunities. Become unforgettable. Preferably while standing next to a motivational roll-up banner.

That version of networking always felt slightly exhausting.

I am much more interested in the practical version:

- conversations about careers that are honest instead of performative
- discussions about current trends without pretending every trend is a revolution
- exchange about tools, methods, failures, trade-offs, and client reality
- meeting people who are actually curious about the work

That is usually where the useful part starts.

## The best signals are rarely in polished slides

Some of the most useful professional insight appears in side conversations.

Not in the official keynote. Not in the seventh thought-leadership post of the week. In the conversation afterward, when someone says:

> We tried this in a client environment and the real bottleneck was identity.

or:

> The tool was fine. The operating model was the actual problem.

That is valuable because it is grounded. It comes with friction, trade-offs, and context. Real practitioners tend to talk in implementation details, not just in slogans.

```txt
Trend hype:     "AI will transform everything."
Useful version: "Fine. Who owns the prompts, the approvals, the audit trail, and the failure mode?"
```

Those are the conversations I want more of.

## So where do you even start?

That is the awkward part, especially if you dislike performative networking culture.

You do not need to become a walking personal-brand strategy. You also do not need to sell the next secret formula for career acceleration, trading success, or consultant enlightenment through a suspicious online course.

Usually, a better starting point is much smaller:

- go to a meetup
- stay for the after-work part
- ask one real question
- talk to people who are visibly interested in the same problems

Not every exchange has to become an opportunity. Some conversations are simply useful because they sharpen your view.

Others become friendships, project ideas, technical sparring partnerships, or unexpected doors later on.

## Why this matters to me

I want more from work than task completion.

I want perspective. I want technical sharpness. I want to stay close to what is current without being dragged around by every hype cycle. And I want to share that space with people who also care about doing solid work.

Networking, in that sense, is not a side quest. It is one of the ways consulting stays alive as a craft.

Because yes, utilization figures matter. Career steps matter. Certifications can matter.

But if all of that happens without real exchange, the job becomes oddly flat.

And if I only wanted to close tickets and disappear, I could probably have picked a different path.

For me, part of consulting is exactly this: learning in public, thinking with others, and occasionally discussing the latest hot technology over a drink without pretending it will save civilization.

That seems like a good place to start.
